Common Questions & Different Approaches

7 key questions below · Full FAQ with 115 research-backed questions on GitHub (use Ctrl+F to search)

"Why not just compare investment returns vs. home appreciation?"

Because that misses the three biggest forces: leverage (you control a $400K asset with $80K down — a 5× multiplier), transaction costs (6% selling + 3% closing = ~$36K on that same home), and tax treatment ($500K cap gains exclusion on a primary residence vs. 15% on investment profits). A pure returns comparison omits all three.

"Shouldn't taxes just be excluded? They change too often."

Taxes don't change the direction as much as people expect — but the $500K primary residence exclusion is massive and stable (enacted 1997, unchanged since). The real issue is the mortgage interest deduction: post-TCJA, the $30K standard deduction means most homeowners get $0 incremental tax benefit. This model captures that correctly. Most online calculators don't.

"Why does renting win so often at default settings?"

Two reasons. First, the default assumes 100% renter discipline — every dollar the renter saves gets invested. Try 70% (the "Renter Discipline" slider) for a reality check. Second, at current mortgage rates (~6%), there's a 4% spread between what stocks return (10% CAGR) and what the mortgage costs. That gap compounds. Change either rate and watch the verdict flip.

"I did my own math and got different numbers."

Great — check which variables differ. The sensitivity tornado (chart dropdown → "What matters most") shows which single assumption drives your result. Usually it's investment return, appreciation, or discipline. If your algebra gives the same core equation (down payment compounded vs. home appreciation + monthly surplus compounded), you've independently validated the model. The simulation adds month-precise amortization, tax treatment, and transaction costs on top of that foundation.

"$3K/month in rent gets a nicer place than $3K/month on a mortgage."

True in many markets, especially in 2024–2026 with rates above 6%. That's the "imputed rent" gap — the owner consumes housing services worth more than their payment buys. This model isolates the financial question only: which path builds more wealth? Quality-of-life differences (space, stability, customization, landlord risk) are real but not quantifiable. They're documented in "What the Model Doesn't Capture" on GitHub.

"Can I trust a model this simple?"

It's not simple — it runs 780 monthly amortization steps per simulation, 4 scenarios simultaneously, and 12 sensitivity perturbations. What it IS is transparent: every assumption is editable, every default is sourced, and every formula is documented. The academic foundations (Poterba, Jordà, Bernstein-Koudijs) are linked in Sources. The full math audit is on GitHub. Verify anything you want.

"What should I actually do with this?"

Enter YOUR numbers (real rent, real savings, real timeline). Look at the verdict. Then break it: slide the discipline to 70%, try a different rate, change the sell year. The goal isn't to get one answer — it's to understand which assumptions your decision depends on and how sensitive it is to being wrong about them.

Common Questions & Different Approaches

7 key questions below · Full FAQ with 115 research-backed questions on GitHub (use Ctrl+F to search)

"Why not just compare investment returns vs. home appreciation?"

Because that misses the three biggest forces: leverage (you control a $400K asset with $80K down — a 5× multiplier), transaction costs (6% selling + 3% closing = ~$36K on that same home), and tax treatment ($500K cap gains exclusion on a primary residence vs. 15% on investment profits). A pure returns comparison omits all three.

"Shouldn't taxes just be excluded? They change too often."

Taxes don't change the direction as much as people expect — but the $500K primary residence exclusion is massive and stable (enacted 1997, unchanged since). The real issue is the mortgage interest deduction: post-TCJA, the $30K standard deduction means most homeowners get $0 incremental tax benefit. This model captures that correctly. Most online calculators don't.

"Why does renting win so often at default settings?"

Two reasons. First, the default assumes 100% renter discipline — every dollar the renter saves gets invested. Try 70% (the "Renter Discipline" slider) for a reality check. Second, at current mortgage rates (~6%), there's a 4% spread between what stocks return (10% CAGR) and what the mortgage costs. That gap compounds. Change either rate and watch the verdict flip.

"I did my own math and got different numbers."

Great — check which variables differ. The sensitivity tornado (chart dropdown → "What matters most") shows which single assumption drives your result. Usually it's investment return, appreciation, or discipline. If your algebra gives the same core equation (down payment compounded vs. home appreciation + monthly surplus compounded), you've independently validated the model. The simulation adds month-precise amortization, tax treatment, and transaction costs on top of that foundation.

"$3K/month in rent gets a nicer place than $3K/month on a mortgage."

True in many markets, especially in 2024–2026 with rates above 6%. That's the "imputed rent" gap — the owner consumes housing services worth more than their payment buys. This model isolates the financial question only: which path builds more wealth? Quality-of-life differences (space, stability, customization, landlord risk) are real but not quantifiable. Monthly-Payment-First: Input payment + down. Engine backsolves: L = M × [(1+r)^n − 1] / [r × (1+r)^n]. Home price = L + down. Same payment at different rates = different home prices.

Month-Precise Amortization: Interest = Balance × (Rate/12), Principal = Payment − Interest. Matches bank schedules exactly.

Tax Benefit — Excess Itemization (post-TCJA): max(0, Itemized − Std Deduction) × Rate. The $30K MFJ standard deduction means most homeowners get $0 incremental benefit. This is the #1 error in online calculators. Non-US countries correctly produce $0 (marginalTax = 0). NL/SE have partial deductions.

Symmetric Surplus Investing: BOTH buyer and renter invest surplus cash. After mortgage payoff, the buyer's freed-up cash gets invested — not ignored like in most calculators.

Geometric Returns (10%, not 12%): Volatility drag: g ≈ a − σ²/2. With σ ≈ 20%, stocks lose ~2% to compounding friction. 10% CAGR is mathematically correct for deterministic models. 12% overstates by ~50% over 30 years. (Jacquier, Kane, Marcus FAJ 2003)

Verdict Confidence: |advantage| / home price. <2% too close, <5% marginal, <15% moderate, ≥15% strong.

Common Questions & Different Approaches

7 key questions below · Full FAQ with 115 research-backed questions on GitHub (use Ctrl+F to search)

"Why not just compare investment returns vs. home appreciation?"

Because that misses the three biggest forces: leverage (you control a $400K asset with $80K down — a 5× multiplier), transaction costs (6% selling + 3% closing = ~$36K on that same home), and tax treatment ($500K cap gains exclusion on a primary residence vs. 15% on investment profits). A pure returns comparison omits all three.

"Shouldn't taxes just be excluded? They change too often."

Taxes don't change the direction as much as people expect — but the $500K primary residence exclusion is massive and stable (enacted 1997, unchanged since). The real issue is the mortgage interest deduction: post-TCJA, the $30K standard deduction means most homeowners get $0 incremental tax benefit. This model captures that correctly. Most online calculators don't.

"Why does renting win so often at default settings?"

Two reasons. First, the default assumes 100% renter discipline — every dollar the renter saves gets invested. Try 70% (the "Renter Discipline" slider) for a reality check. Second, at current mortgage rates (~6%), there's a 4% spread between what stocks return (10% CAGR) and what the mortgage costs. That gap compounds. Change either rate and watch the verdict flip.

"I did my own math and got different numbers."

Great — check which variables differ. The sensitivity tornado (chart dropdown → "What matters most") shows which single assumption drives your result. Usually it's investment return, appreciation, or discipline. If your algebra gives the same core equation (down payment compounded vs. home appreciation + monthly surplus compounded), you've independently validated the model. The simulation adds month-precise amortization, tax treatment, and transaction costs on top of that foundation.

"$3K/month in rent gets a nicer place than $3K/month on a mortgage."

True in many markets, especially in 2024–2026 with rates above 6%. That's the "imputed rent" gap — the owner consumes housing services worth more than their payment buys. This model isolates the financial question only: which path builds more wealth? Quality-of-life differences (space, stability, customization, landlord risk) are real but not quantifiable. They're documented in "What the Model Doesn't Capture" on GitHub.

"Can I trust a model this simple?"

It's not simple — it runs 780 monthly amortization steps per simulation, 4 scenarios simultaneously, and 12 sensitivity perturbations. What it IS is transparent: every assumption is editable, every default is sourced, and every formula is documented. The academic foundations (Poterba, Jordà, Bernstein-Koudijs) are linked in Sources. The full math audit is on GitHub. Verify anything you want.

"What should I actually do with this?"

Enter YOUR numbers (real rent, real savings, real timeline). Look at the verdict. Then break it: slide the discipline to 70%, try a different rate, change the sell year. The goal isn't to get one answer — it's to understand which assumptions your decision depends on and how sensitive it is to being wrong about them.
